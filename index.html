Operating Style
How I actually work
01
I build the infrastructure
When a program has no blueprint, I don't wait for one. I design the systems, recruit the people, write the documentation, and build the workflows — so what didn't exist yesterday is operational by next quarter.
02
I hold complexity without dropping it
Concurrent programs, overlapping stakeholders, moving deadlines. I think in systems — tracking how pieces connect before they collide, and tightening the seams others miss.
03
I diagnose in real time
Programs reveal their gaps mid-flight. I don't wait for the retrospective — I spot what's breaking, design a fix, and ship it before the problem compounds.
04
Cross-cultural fluency is my default mode
Four working languages. Work across multiple world regions. I design programs knowing what actually derails global delivery culturally — and build against those failure points from day one.
05
I move at the speed the work demands
Grant cycles don't pause. Launches don't slide. I plan against the timeline I have, not the one I'd prefer — and I'm accountable for outcomes, not activity.
→

Program architecture for research ecosystems that don't exist yet. AI governance where the stakes are real. Multilingual access as infrastructure, not afterthought. Global scholar pipelines in places the field hasn't reached. I'm not looking for a lane — I'm building one.

I've never managed a program that had a template. Every single one I've built — the infrastructure, the onboarding, the recovery plan when things went sideways — I made from scratch. That's not a limitation. That's the job.

AI Governance, Policy & Public Programming
Society for Research in Child Development
Co-developed SRCD's Generative AI Policy (adopted August 2024) governing authorship, disclosure, and peer review integrity across all four journals — then translated the policy into a multi-year public research agenda through interdisciplinary AI webinars reaching 300+ registrants.
AI PolicyResearch EthicsPublic ProgrammingGovernance
+ Expand
Not just drafting the policy — operationalizing it. Turning SRCD's position on AI into living governance and a public scholarly record the field can build on.
The situation
As generative AI reshaped scholarly publishing and research, SRCD needed both a formal governance framework and a public-facing research agenda. Translating a rapidly moving, contested landscape into actionable policy across disciplines, career stages, and global contexts.
My role
Co-developer of SRCD's Generative AI Policy (adopted by Governing Council, August 2024). Staff liaison for the interdisciplinary AI programming series — 2024 through 2026 and continuing.
What I actually did
  • Co-developed SRCD's AI policy: authorship standards, disclosure requirements, hallucination risk, IP — governing all four journals since August 2024
  • Organized the 2024 webinar Is Technology Good or Bad for Child Development? — 300+ registrants, September 2024
  • Coordinating the May 2026 webinar Advancing Research on AI and Child Development — algorithmic bias, digital equity, inclusive design, globally underrepresented populations
  • Connecting policy to programming: ensuring the field has both the governance framework and the research agenda to engage AI responsibly
What came out of it
SRCD AI Policy live across all publications since August 2024. A connected interdisciplinary webinar series building the field's public scholarly record on AI ethics, equity, and child development.
What this signals
Operational AI governance experience where getting it wrong has real consequences — not theoretical, not adjacent. Paired with public programming that shows the policy isn't paperwork; it's a framework the field is actively building on.
2024 – Present
Language-Sensitive Review (LSR) Pathway
SRCD · Robert Wood Johnson Foundation · Oxford University Press
Architected a first-of-its-kind, grant-funded equity intervention in scholarly peer review for Spanish- and Chinese-speaking researchers — from project charter through ScholarOne workflow configuration, a 35-member bilingual reviewer pool, and full OUP platform launch.
EquityGrant-FundedSystems DesignOUP
+ Expand
Designing a structural equity intervention in peer review with no precedent — from grant to implementation, inside a major publisher's submission infrastructure.
The situation
Spanish- and Chinese-speaking researchers face structural barriers at the peer review stage of scholarly publishing. SRCD needed a program that addressed the problem at the workflow level — not just aspirationally — with Robert Wood Johnson Foundation funding and Oxford University Press as the implementation partner.
My role
Program architect. Owned the full lifecycle from grant to live pilot: project charter, ScholarOne configuration, reviewer pool recruitment, member communications, survey instruments, and editorial onboarding.
What I actually did
  • Authored the project charter and led ScholarOne workflow configuration with OUP's submissions systems team
  • Recruited and tiered a 35-member bilingual reviewer pool (Spanish and Chinese)
  • Developed member-facing communications, survey instruments, and editorial onboarding materials
  • Piloted as a novel workflow configuration inside ScholarOne's editor-side infrastructure — a first for the platform
  • Targeted Child Development and Child Development Perspectives as pilot journals
What came out of it
A live, grant-funded equity pilot embedded in OUP's submission infrastructure. First-of-its-kind workflow configuration in ScholarOne. Operational infrastructure positioned for expansion to additional journals and language communities.
What this signals
Grant-to-implementation program architecture. The ability to translate an equity commitment into a technical workflow, a funded program structure, and a live operational system — inside a major academic publisher's infrastructure.

Fellowship ManagementProgram DesignSole PMIteration

Solo program managing a competitive interdisciplinary fellowship across three full cohort cycles — and redesigning it between them because the program needed to evolve.
The situation
Run the full lifecycle of a competitive fellowship — solo — across three cycles. After 2023, I identified the program needed a formal mentorship layer. So I built one for 2025 and carried it into 2026.
My role
Sole program manager for all three cycles. Every aspect of design, recruitment, selection, onboarding, mentor matching, programming, and public communications — mine, across all three cohorts.

38 fellows and mentors supported across three cohorts. Zero support staff. A more sophisticated program model each year — designed, built, iterated, and owned by one person.
